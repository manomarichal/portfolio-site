About troll levels


A "troll" level is a level that tries to predict what the player will do and punishes them for it. A good one does that in an entertaining manner while still being easy to progress through once the player has been "trolled". This makes the playtesting and revision process a crucial step, both to make sure a level's predictions actually match what players do and to refine the cues the level gives the player so they don't get lost. This type of level is something of a happy accident resulting from the simplistic complexity of the Mario Maker games, and are a great example of what it means to push an engine to it's limits. Over the past six years, a large community has grown around them, of which I've had the pleasure to be a part of.

Many troll levels, including mine use glitches to help subverting expectations, and as Nintendo is not a fan of these, troll levels usually get deleted, hence only the only thing remaining is videos.

D&D: Volcanic Panic!


A Dungeons and Dragons one-shot I am currently writing. In this adventure, players explores a dormant volcano to confirm a rumor that the Ancient Red Dragon Sezyryn has passed. The party will have to fight their way through a two-story tall dungeon, filled to the brim with deadly traps and monsters. It is inspired by one of my favorite one-shots, Mines of Madness, and takes somewhere around 4-6 hours to play. It is still very much a work-in-progress.

The unique part of this adventure is that players traverse the dungeon in two directions; after reaching the heart of the volcano, Sezyryn with his last breath causes the volcano to erupt. The party now has to make their way through the dungeon backwards to the entrance, while being chased by rising lava levels. However, the eruption caused some changes; the once reposeful hot springs where you could relax and heal while descending might have turned into a boiling obstacle, and so on.

Dungeon of the Mad Dungeon Master


A boardgame prototype designed by me and some friends. In this game, a party of adventurers is desperately trying to escape an ever changing dungeon. One player takes up the role as the dungeon master, while the rest play as adventurers. However, unlike Dungeons and Dragons, in this game the dungeon master's goal is to actually kill the players by creating the deadliest dungeon possible. Adventurers will face monsters, activate traps, obtain treasure and traverse different biomes. The fun doesn't stop when the players die, they turn into ghosts that can still help their allies from the other side.

This was the first boardgame I tried to design, which was interesting. Boardgames heavily rely on player interaction, and intuitive but challenging enough strategic design to provide a mentally stimulating experience. Hence, a lot of time was spent playtesting the various mechanics

The game unfortunately never made it past making one physical version :(

Electric Alps


The game I worked on as an intern Game Design at Dirty Paws Studio. I designed the progression & reward systems the game will feature, and blockouts and the mechanical progression of the tutorial.

Designing these systems was a very good exercise in communication, as I joined a team in progress and had to understand their vision and what the various systems I was designing had to achieve. I went through many iterations of designing, asking and integrating feedback. In the end, I designed the overall progression of the game and the various reward and progression systems that the game will feature.

The tutorial was challenging as well. Electric Alps is a puzzle game, for which properly teaching players is vital. The tutorial should also be enjoyable, and teach players naturally while playing the game. I ended up sketching 12 blockouts of levels that teach the player the various mechanics in a natural manner. I would love to show off the GDD or levels but they are still a secret.
